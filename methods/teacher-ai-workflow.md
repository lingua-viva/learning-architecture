# Teacher AI Workflow: How I Actually Use AI in My Practice

**Status**: `validated` — daily use since 2024

---

## Philosophy

> **The implementation and the human interaction happening in class is still 90% of the impact. AI supports the 10% that is structural — planning, materials, documentation. The teacher is still the teacher.**

AI does not replace teachers. AI supports the structural work that takes teachers away from teaching.

The critical thinking, the empathy, the connection through relationship, the complexity of reading a classroom in real time — these are things only humans can fully grasp and have an impact on. Students need to be seen by their teacher as a role model, a guide, someone close to them who understands them in their actions and their thinking, who guides them through difficult times and celebrates their success, and who makes mistakes and explicitly teaches how important mistakes are.

What AI can do is handle the structural load: planning, differentiating materials, organizing assessments, drafting communications, and structuring learning experiences. This frees me to do the work that only I can do — the relational, responsive, human work of teaching.

## Real Workflows

### 1. Planning a Learning Experience with Claude

**The prompt pattern**: I share my planner context — grade level, IB transdisciplinary theme, time available, student language levels — and ask Claude to design a learning experience using Understanding by Design (UbD) and the Six Facets of Understanding.

**Example prompt** (anonymized):
> "Help me plan by design considering the Six Facets of Understanding. I'm teaching a third-grade classroom in an IB language immersion, Reggio-inspired school. The transdisciplinary theme is 'How We Organize Ourselves.' I have 6 sessions of 45 minutes each. Students' Italian proficiency ranges from CEFR A1 to A2. The unit should integrate Italian Ministerial Standards for Grade 3 reading comprehension."

**What Claude gives me**: A structured plan with learning objectives mapped to each Facet, suggested activities sequenced across the 6 sessions, differentiation notes for A1 vs A2 students, and suggested assessment evidence.

**What I change**: Almost always the pacing (Claude underestimates transition time with 8-year-olds), the specific activities (I know my students' interests and energy patterns), and the assessment format (I prefer authentic performance tasks over the written tasks Claude defaults to).

**Why this matters**: Before AI, this planning process took me an evening. With Claude, the structural design takes 20 minutes. I spend the saved time on the part that matters more — thinking about specific students, anticipating challenges, preparing differentiated materials.

### 2. Creating Customized Vocabulary Materials

**The prompt pattern**: I give a topic and ask for a vocabulary list calibrated to my students' level, then transform it into printable materials.

**Example prompt**:
> "Create a vocabulary list of 15 words related to [unit topic] appropriate for Italian CEFR A1-A2 learners in Grade 3. Include the word, a simple Italian definition, and a visual description I can use to create flashcards. Group by difficulty."

**What I get**: A structured list that I can immediately convert to printable cards for classroom use.

**What I adjust**: I always verify that the words match what my students actually encounter in the unit. AI sometimes selects vocabulary that is technically correct but not contextually relevant to how we'll explore the topic.

### 3. Report Card Narratives

**The prompt pattern**: I draft bullet points about a student's progress, then ask AI to create a professional narrative that communicates clearly to families while maintaining the warm, specific tone I want.

**What this solves**: Writing 25 individualized narratives per semester is one of the most time-consuming tasks in teaching. AI handles the prose structure while I ensure the content is accurate and the voice is mine.

**What I never delegate**: The observations themselves. AI writes the narrative from my notes — it does not generate observations about students it has never seen.

### 4. Email Drafting and Communication

**The prompt pattern**: I draft the core message, then ask AI to adjust the tone — more formal for leadership communications, warmer for family outreach, more structured for colleague coordination.

**Why this matters for a multilingual educator**: I communicate professionally in Italian, English, and French. AI helps me ensure that each communication lands with the appropriate register and cultural tone for its audience. This is not about fixing grammar — it is about cultural calibration.

## What Teachers Get Wrong About AI

The biggest mistake I see is the assumption that AI can generate anything and solve problems by itself. It cannot. The prompt, the questioning, the validation process — these require human critical sense. AI tends to please you no matter what. If you don't know what you want concretely before interacting, you will get a polished version of nothing useful.

**My first lesson if I were training 100 teachers**:

Don't underestimate that you are still the expert. You need to validate whatever AI generates. The importance of the prompt is real, but so is the reiteration — asking again more specifically always produces better results.

Start by knowing what you want concretely before interacting, or use AI for a preliminary exploration phase to survey topics, resources, and research. Then refine.

The most important thing for teachers to understand: **using AI does not diminish your contribution**. The implementation and the human interaction happening in class is still 90% of the impact. You can have the best lesson plan ever, but if you cannot manage your class, understand your students' needs, feel the room, or deal with their emotional states, the learning will not happen effectively.

AI makes the 10% that is structural — planning, materials, documentation — dramatically better. The 90% that is human remains yours.

## What I Would Build If I Had Engineering Support

1. **An observation-to-profile pipeline**: Voice recording → AI transcription → structured student profile that accumulates year over year. The technology exists (Toddle AI, Whisper, etc.). The design challenge is the observation protocol — what to record, when, in what format, so the output is genuinely useful for the next teacher.

2. **A curriculum-aware planning assistant**: An AI tool that knows my school's specific curriculum framework (IB PYP scope and sequence + CEFR targets + Reggio documentation expectations) and can generate planning suggestions that honor all three. Current tools know IB or know language learning, but none know how they interact in an immersion context.

3. **A formative assessment synthesizer**: Something that takes my Kahoot data, Toddle portfolio entries, and CEFR assessments and shows me a unified view of each student's trajectory — not three separate dashboards, but one integrated picture.

These are not engineering challenges. They are learning design challenges that require engineering to implement. The hardest part is not building the tool — it is knowing what the tool should do, because you have lived the problem it solves.
