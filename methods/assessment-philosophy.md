# Assessment Philosophy: Measuring What Matters

**Status**: `validated` — 93% improvement measured against national norms (2024-25)

---

## Principle

Every instructional decision should be traceable to evidence. Not compliance evidence — learning evidence. The question is not "did the student complete the task?" but "is the student genuinely more capable than they were before?"

---

## The Assessment Stack

I use four layers of assessment, each measuring something different:

### Layer 1: CEFR Progression (Language Development)

The Common European Framework of Reference for Languages provides a structured proficiency scale from A1 (beginner) to B2 (upper intermediate). I track each student's Italian proficiency across four skills — listening, speaking, reading, writing — using CEFR-aligned rubrics.

**What this tells me**: Where each student is on their language journey and whether they are progressing at an appropriate rate. A student who is A1 in reading but A2 in speaking has a specific profile that calls for specific support — not a generic "needs improvement."

**How I track it**: Custom Google Sheets dashboards showing CEFR progression vertically across G1-G5, with grade-level and cross-campus comparison graphs. This makes growth visible and comparable.

### Layer 2: Prove MT National Benchmarking (Standardized Outcomes)

Prove MT is Italy's national standardized assessment for reading comprehension. It is designed for native Italian speakers — meaning my students, who are second-language learners in an immersion context, are measured against the toughest possible benchmark.

**2024-25 Results** (Grade 3):
- 93% of students improved reading comprehension over the academic year
- 100% of students who started at or below average improved by year end
- Median comprehension score advanced from 5.0 to 8.0 out of 10
- 33% of students reached a perfect score of 10/10
- Every learner in the cohort progressed. No exceptions.

**Why this matters**: These are L2 learners measured against native norms. The improvement is not because the test was easy — it is because the instructional approach (structured immersion with differentiated support) works. This is the evidence that the curriculum architecture produces real outcomes.

**What I attribute this to**: My teaching practice and the structured approach to immersion — not AI. AI supports my planning, but the learning happens in the classroom through human interaction, carefully designed activities, and responsive instruction. I want to be precise about this claim.

### Layer 3: Formative Assessment (Real-Time Feedback)

Kahoot quizzes for Italian comprehension — ongoing, frequent, low-stakes. Students love the format. I get immediate data on who understood what, without the anxiety of formal testing.

Toddle portfolios — student work documented with teacher annotations, visible to families. This is Reggio-inspired documentation made practical: the portfolio is both evidence of learning and a communication tool.

### Layer 4: Authentic Performance (Transfer Evidence)

The deepest assessment: can the student use what they learned in a context they haven't seen before? This shows up in transdisciplinary projects where students apply Italian language skills to real-world scenarios — economics, ethics, community engagement.

One example: a unit integrating economics, ethics, and real-world market experience where students had to negotiate, present, and reflect — all in Italian. The learning was visible not in a test score but in the quality of their reasoning and their ability to function in Italian in an unscripted situation.

---

## Design Principles for Assessment

1. **Multiple evidence sources**: No single assessment tells the whole story. CEFR tracks proficiency. Prove MT benchmarks against national standards. Formative assessment catches gaps in real time. Performance tasks show transfer. Together, they create a comprehensive picture.

2. **Data serves instruction**: I do not collect data for reporting. I collect data to make better instructional decisions. If an assessment result does not change what I do next, it is not worth the time it takes.

3. **Transparency with families**: Families see Toddle portfolios, CEFR progress, and narrative reports. They know where their child is, where they are going, and what the evidence says. This builds trust and reinforces families' confidence in the program's consistency.

4. **Honest about what is measured and what is not**: Prove MT measures reading comprehension. It does not measure oral fluency, cultural competence, creative expression, or the ability to function in Italian in real life. These matter too — they are just harder to measure. I name this gap rather than pretending the metrics capture everything.
