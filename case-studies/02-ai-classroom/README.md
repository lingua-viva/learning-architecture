# AI in the Classroom: An Institutional Proposal

**Status**: `designed` · `proposed` to institutional leadership (March 2026)

---

## The Problem: Continuity

When a student moves from Grade 2 to Grade 3, the new teacher starts from scratch.

Learning styles, what worked, what didn't, which accommodations helped, which interventions were tried — this knowledge lives in the previous teacher's head. With teacher turnover, it disappears entirely. Students with specific learning needs are particularly affected: they spend the first weeks of each year being re-assessed for things that were already understood.

This is not a technology problem. It is a structural problem — Layer 5 (Documentation & Institutional Memory) of the [Structural Coherence Framework](../01-structural-coherence/). But AI offers a specific, practical solution.

---

## The Proposal: Two Systems, One Student

### System 1: Teacher Observation Lenses

**What it is**: A digital profile for every student that follows them K-8. When a teacher opens a student's profile, they see learning style preferences, academic strengths and growth areas, language balance by CEFR level, what interventions and strategies worked, social-emotional observations, accommodation needs, and interest maps.

**How it works**: Teachers record 2-3 minute voice notes about each student at key moments (end of unit, end of semester, year-end handoff). AI transcribes and summarizes these observations into the student profile. The profile accumulates year over year. New teachers read the profile before school starts.

**What it replaces**: The current system where institutional knowledge about students lives in individual teachers' heads and leaves when they do.

**What it does NOT do**: It does not assess students. It does not make instructional decisions. It does not interact with students. It is a teacher-facing documentation tool that uses AI to reduce the burden of writing while preserving the richness of observation.

### System 2: Tiered Adaptive Support

**Three tiers**, matched to student need:

| Tier | What | For Whom |
|------|------|----------|
| **Universal** | Adaptive practice platforms for math and reading | All students K-8 |
| **Targeted** | AI reading support and content leveling tools | Students below grade level |
| **Intensive** | Personalized learning paths designed with specialist input | Students with diagnosed learning differences |

**The key principle**: The tier a student receives is determined by teacher observation and assessment data, not by the AI system. Teachers decide. AI supports.

---

## Why AI — and Why Not

### Where AI helps
- **Reducing documentation burden**: Voice-to-text transcription and summarization saves 20-35 minutes per documentation session (based on research from comparable tools like Toddle AI and Branching Minds).
- **Preserving nuance at scale**: A teacher's observation that "a student does better with visual supports in the afternoon but can handle auditory input in the morning" is exactly the kind of knowledge that gets lost in transitions. AI transcription preserves it.
- **Adaptive practice**: Platforms that adjust difficulty to the student's actual level (not their grade level) keep students in the zone of proximal development without requiring the teacher to create 20 different worksheets.

### Where AI does not help
- **Relational knowledge**: AI cannot replicate the trust a student has in a specific teacher, or the intuition that comes from watching a child navigate a challenging social situation.
- **Pedagogical judgment**: Whether to push a student or give them space, whether to intervene in a conflict or let students resolve it, whether today's frustration is productive or harmful — these remain human decisions.
- **The 90% that matters**: The implementation and the human interaction happening in class is still 90% of the impact of the lesson. AI supports the 10% that is structural — documentation, practice differentiation, data continuity. The teacher is still the teacher.

## What Happened

I presented this proposal to school leadership in March 2026. Leadership was appreciative but is processing institutional changes one at a time. The concrete response has been to expand my role as Italian Program Coordinator and support the development of a new curriculum framework — which suggests the structural thinking is valued, even if the AI-specific proposal has not yet been implemented.

This is the reality of institutional change in education: good ideas move at the speed of organizational readiness, not at the speed of the person who had the idea. I consider this proposal a foundation document that will become relevant as the school's capacity for change grows.

---

## What I Would Build Next

If I were designing this system with engineering support, I would start with System 1 (teacher observation lenses) because it requires no student-facing AI, no parental consent for AI interaction, and minimal privacy risk. The technical requirements are straightforward: voice recording → transcription → summarization → structured profile storage. The hard part is not the technology — it is designing the observation protocol so that teacher input is efficient, consistent, and genuinely useful for the next teacher who reads it.

This is a learning design problem, not an engineering problem. And that is where my experience is.
